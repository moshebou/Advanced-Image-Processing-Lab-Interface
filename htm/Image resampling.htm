8         Image resampling

8.1         Signals Zoom

8.1.1     Theoretical background [1]

Image re-sampling assumes reconstruction of a continuous approximation of the original non-sampled image by means of interpolation of available image samples to obtain samples “in-between” the available ones. The most feasible is interpolation by means of digital filtering implemented as digital convolution. A number of convolutional interpolation methods are known, beginning from the simplest and the least accurate nearest neighbor and linear (bilinear, for 2D case) interpolations to more accurate cubic (bicubic, for 2D case) and higher order spline methods.

Discrete sinc-interpolation is the gold standard for re-sampling sampled data.

8.2         Image Zoom

8.2.1     Theoretical background

The 2D Nearest neighbor interpolation, which Like its 1D counterpart, selects the value of the nearest point and does not consider the values of neighboring points at all, yielding a piecewise-constant interpolant.

The bilinear interpolation, which is the 2D counterpart of the 1D linear-interpolation. It calculates the current pixel value as a weighted sum of its 4 nearest neighbors, and can be calculated as a 2 passes of 1D linear interpolation – one along the y axis, and the other along the x axis of the result.

The bi-cubic interpolation, which is the 2D counterpart of the 1D cubic-interpolation. It calculates the current pixel value as a weighted sum of its 16 nearest neighbors, and can be calculated as a 2 passes of 1D cubic interpolation – one along the y axis, and the other along the x axis of the result.

And the 2D sinc interpolation, which is the 2D counterpart of the 1D sinc interpolation. It can be calculated as a 2 passes of 1D sinc interpolation – one along the y axis, and the other along the x axis of the result.

All of those kernels point spread function can be represented as multiplication of 1D version of the point spread functions.
