5.1.1     Theoretical background

5.1.1.1     Statistical properties of images [1]

Probabilistic models do not specify individual signals of an ensemble. Rather they specify only certain signal statistical characteristics, i.e. averages over the ensemble of certain signal attributes. In this averaging, it is most natural to associate contribution of individual signals to the ensemble average, which are embodied in weight coefficients and weighting function, with the rate of occurrence in realizations of individual elements of the ensemble. The mathematical models assume usually that the number of realization of signals is infinitely large, and this rate is called probability, or, in continuous case, probability density.

The most fundamental notion of probabilistic models is that of a random variable.  Quantized (discrete) random variable  is statistically specified by probability distribution  of its values, where  is an integer index,  and  is the total number of possible quantized values.  Probability distribution  is a limit, when the number of realizations of the variable goes to infinity, of the rates of individual values  in a finite set of realizations. The set of these rates is called the distribution histogram of the variable and the rates for particular values of   are called histogram bins. The number of occurrences of individual values  in a finite set of realizations is called the cardinality of the set of these values.  The sum of histogram bins from minimal value   of the variable to a particular value  is called the cumulative histogram.

Continuous random variables  are specified by the probability  that the variable has a value lower than. This probability as a function of  is called the cumulative distribution function. It is defined as a limit of the cumulative histogram, when the number of realizations and the number of levels of random variable both go to infinity. Its derivative is called the probability density. The probability density can be regarded as a limit of histogram bins of random variable, when the number of its quantized levels goes to infinity.

5.1.1.2     Local adaptive filtering [2]

Given an image to be processed, filtering can be designed and carried out either over the entire available image frame or fragment-wise. The former is referred as global filtering and to the latter as local filtering.

Applicability of global and local filtering depends on whether images belong to classes of spatial homogeneous or spatial inhomogeneous images. In spatial homogeneous image local spectra vary not substantially, otherwise images are inhomogeneous. 

Global filtering is suited for spatial homogeneous images. For spatial inhomogeneous filtering, local filtering is required.

The theoretical framework for local filtering is provided by local criteria of processing quality that evaluate the processing quality individually over image fragments centered at each pixel.

The most straightforward way to implement local filtering is to perform it in a “hopping” window. Hopping window processing being very attractive from the computational complexity point of view suffers however from blocking effects, artifacts in a form of discontinuities at the edges of the hopping window. Obviously, the ultimate solution of the ‘‘blocking effects’’ problem would be sliding window processing.

In the sliding window filtering, one should, for each position of the filter window, compute transform coefficients of the signal within window, determine on this base the filter coefficients, modify accordingly image transform coefficients and then compute inverse transform. With sliding window, inverse transform need not, in principle, be computed for all pixels within window since only the central sample of the window has to be determined in order to form the output image in the process of image scanning with the filter window.

All described transforms can be applied “globally” to entire set of available signal samples or “locally” to individual signal fragments. In the latter case, signal is split into non-overlapping or overlapping fragments, and transformation is performed over each fragment individually. 5.6         Switch Magnitude and Phase of Image Spectra

5.6.1     Theoretical background

From numerous experiments it is known, that the spectral magnitude of a realistic image is not very informative. It contains information about distribution of the signal energy in the frequency domain. Exchanging the spectral magnitudes of two similar images belonging to the same class and perform the inverse Fourier transform, then the difference in visual appearance of the original and obtained images will be negligible [4].

5.6.2     Experiment Description

The “Switch Magnitude and Phase of Image Spectra” experiment, as its name indicates, takes two images, calculate their DFTs, and switch their magnitudes.

5.6.3     Results

Figure ‎5‑6: “Switch Magnitude and Phase of Image Spectra”. Top left is the first image. Bottom left is the second image. Top right is the restored image of second image magnitude and first image phase combination. Bottom right is the restored image of first image magnitude and second image phase combination.

Figure ‎5‑6 displayed result support the empirical claim that most of the information in an image conveyed in its spectra phase component rather than in the magnitude. In displayed examples the faces are clearly noticeable even the magnitude of the spectra is taken from other image. There are small nuances in the image which are hidden in the spectrum amplitude, such as the nose at the bottom right image of Figure ‎5‑6.
