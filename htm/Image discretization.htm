1.1.1.1     Discretization

Signal digitization is carried out in two steps: discretization and element-wise quantization.

At the first step of the digitization, called signal discretization, continuous signals are converted into a set of real numbers that form signal discrete representation. At the second stage, called scalar (element wise) quantization, this set of numbers is, number by number, converted into a set of quantized numbers, which finally results in a digital signal that corresponds to the initial continuous one.

The conversion of continuous signals into discrete ones can be modeled as computing coefficients that represent signals in their expansion over a set of basis functions.

1.1.2     Experiment description

The “Discrete Representation” experiment compares the energy compaction capability of four digital transforms – DFT, DCT, Haar and Walsh, as described at ‎2.1.1.

Energy compaction capability is the capability of transforms to concentrate signal energy in few signal decomposition coefficients.

This experiment also gives a good comparison between the visual effects cause by zeroing smallest absolute value coefficients of each transform.

The experiment procedure is:

The test image is transformed using four different transforms.

The absolute values of the transforms are sorted in descending order.

The coefficients of the smallest absolute value are zeroed until the total energy of the transform reaches to a threshold defined by the user

The image is restored from the result transform coefficients.

1.3      Aliasing 1D

1.3.1     Theoretical background

As described in ‎2.2.1, aliasing is caused by penetrating tails of the pre-filtered signal spectrum periodical replicas inside the signal baseband, if not ideal pre and post filters are used.

The aliasing problem can be explained using 1D signal in the time domain, using Figure ‎2‑8.

Figure ‎2‑8: Aliasing 1D time domain example. There are two different sinusoids that fit the same set of samples.

Figure ‎2‑8 represent two signals – first (red) is a high frequency signal, and the other (blue) with small frequency.  Any sampling of the high frequency signal with smaller than Nyquist sampling rate, will cause any restoration of the signal to be ambiguous, and an interpreter of the sampled data can restore the blue signal instead of the red.

1.3.2     Experiment description

The “Aliasing 1D” experiment illustrates aliasing affect in 1D sampling. Using a general structure of a 1D signal, where K is an increasing constant, and t is the sampling interval; the aliasing can be illustrated when the signal frequency is higher than the sampling rate.

This general structure of the signal, which is composed of a cosine part and a DC part, is useful to examine, since this signal structure in the Fourier transform is comprised out of 3 delta functions – one in the zero frequency, and two others in a frequency determined by the argument of the cosine.

The experiment uses the mentioned signal structure with an increasing parameter K, a displays the signal, its Fourier transform relative to the unstamped Fourier transform with sampling rate indicator, and Fourier image of current and previous signals Fourier transforms.

Once the K value is big enough so the frequency of the signal is higher than the sampling rate, aliasing occurs.

1.4      Image LPF

1.4.1     Theoretical background

As mentioned at ‎2.1 Discrete Representation the capability of transforms to concentrate signal energy in few signal decomposition coefficients is called energy compaction capability.  This property was examined at ‎2.1 Discrete Representation with regards to the absolute value of the coefficients.  Another aspect of energy compaction capability is with regards to coefficient energy, i.e., the more energy a transform have in lower frequencies, the better the transform is.

Theoretically, Karhunen-Loeve transforms and related Hottelling and SVD transform have the best energy compaction capability among linear transforms (Karhunen-Loeve transforms, Hottelling and SVD transform are covered at appendix…). However in practice of image processing they remain to be a theoretical bench mark because of implementation problems and other transforms are used, such as Discrete Fourier, Discrete Cosine, Discrete Walsh and Discrete Haar transforms.

Among those transforms, the DCT , introduced by N. Ahmed, T. Natarajan and K.R. Rao, proved to have a very good energy compaction capability and is very frequently considered as an approximation to Karhunen-Loeve transform.
